On January 2nd, 2014, I started work as Web Designer for the Brooklyn Museum. I haven't yet done any significant work on the website, however, nor am I working on a full site redesign . . . yet. I was brought on to design a project much bigger in scope: the Bloomberg Connects Project, also know as the Visiter Experience Initiative, also known as Ask.

This is my process documentation, explaining the role I've played in the project and the work I've done for the museum. To do this properly, I'll need to take a step back and present some context.

Bloomberg Connects

The full scope of the Bloomberg Connects project is still open, but there are a handful of elements we do know will be included: a new iOS app, a web application to work in tandem with the iOS app, new digital signage for the lobby and galleries, and a full website redesign. Other necessary digital elements may present themselves at any time during the three year timeline, making the roadmap a tentatively scheduled one. We have a full development team in-house, and we're having fun with this.

Everything we're building evolves around our mission statement:

“To create a dynamic and responsive museum that fosters dialogue and sparks conversation between staff and all Museum visitors.”

What we build and how we build it is an ongoing discussion. The one thing that doesn't change is our commitment to the visitor.

The Project Begins

Before my hire, Shelley Bernstein, Sara Devine, and their team had been testing various paper prototypes for months, eventually defining the contours of the project. What they discovered was that our visitors pushed back against additional proscriptive information like more signage or algorithmically generated suggestions. This lead to a new idea: creating an environment which allowed the visitors themselves to approach museum staff with questions about the art.

This is a big leap in the opposite direction from what other museums are doing with their tech. Most museums today are using their apps as another means of over-informing their visitors with secondary media: essays, videos, audio. What we decided to build was an app which gave agency to the visitor over what they hoped to learn. We would empower them with the opportunity to question us directly.

My first day of employment was at this point of the project.

My work began on two fronts. First: researching typefaces, color palettes, and possibly relevant UI/UX patterns. And second: building wireframes for the Host Dashboard and iOS app.

Branding

The Brooklyn Museum branding (and website design) was completed by 2x4 Studios in 2004. Recent visitor research has revealed that our logo and branding continues to be recognizable and likable, so we wouldn't be entertaining any ideas about making updates to it. This did present me with two problems, however -- issues with color and fonts.

The color palette chosen by 2x4 for our print materials and visitor tags, while fun and eye-catching on physical objects, do not translate well to digital use, and especially not for user interface design. Individually, each color looks fine, but when placed together in one view, they clash and work against each other. After a few days experimenting with 2x4 colors and studying other user interface palettes, I came up with a palette which works well with our official brand color -- process cyan -- and play nicely when used together. Black, white, and cyan will still remain the basis for most of our public-facing views. The new palette will be used for all the components of the Ask project.

Introduction

I joined the Brooklyn Museum Technology Department in January 2014, with an official title of Web Designer. When I walked in on my first day, I was preparing myself to talk about the design of a new website. Turned out that what I was hired for was a much bigger project.

The official name of the project is Ask, and it's funded by the Bloomberg Connects grant program. For a full year previous to my hire, Shelley Bernstein and Sara Devine had been testing paper prototypes, eventually defining the contours of the project, allowing the design and development to begin.

The Bloomberg Connects project began in earnest in early March, 2014. (During January and February, I worked on a number of other smaller projects: design directing the Sackler Center Video site (designed by Karyn Campbell), a complete content audit of our current website, small visual updates to our social sites, thoroughly studying our site analytics, getting comfortable with our branding guidelines, etc.) The entire process had so far been iterative, and would continue to be. All progress would evolve around a central mission statement:

“To create a dynamic and responsive museum that fosters dialogue and sparks conversation between staff and all Museum visitors.”

This would involve multiple pieces: an iOS app, digital signage, web applications for our engagement team (hosts, curators, artists, etc.), a full website redesign, and a restructuring of the building's lobby. The project was given a three year roadmap, and we began work on the central feature -- the conversational tool which connects visitors with museum staff. This feature is based around the iOS app and the Host Dashboard.
